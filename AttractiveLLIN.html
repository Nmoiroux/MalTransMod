Attractive LLIN

Nicolas Moiroux

15 March 2019

Results

We simulated the effect of providing human communities with attractive LLINs on malaria transmission in comparison with communities receiving inert or deterrent LLINs. Attractive LLINs used in these simulation induced vector preferences (parameter pLLIN, Table 1) of 0.6 and 0.7: a vector preference for LLINs of 0.6 indicates that 60% of malaria vectors choose a human under an LLIN if presented against an unprotected human. Vector preferences of 0.6 and 0.7 have been observed several times in nature ( and Supplementary Figure 1). The reference inert LLIN induced a vector preference for LLIN of 0.5 (i.e, no preference). Vector preference of the reference deterrent LLIN was set to 0.36 (only 36% of malaria vectors choose a human under an LLIN if presented against an unprotected human) that is the mean preference value among significantly deterrent ITNs in [1]. We also simulated the effect of inert LLINs against the reference deterrent LLIN.

Effect of LLIN coverage

According to our model, attractive LLINs are expected to reduce malaria transmission in comparison to inert or deterrent LLINs whatever the coverage percentage. When all other parameters of the model were set at default values (Table 1), the maximum reduction was reached at 60-61 % coverage. At these coverage rates, an highly attractive LLIN (pLLIN= 0.7) is expected to reduce malaria transmission (expressed as vectorial capacity) by 46.04 % compared to an inert LLIN (Figure 1A) and by 64.5 % compared to a deterrent one (Figure 1B). At 60-61 % coverage, slightly attractive LLINs (pLLIN= 0.6) are expected to reduce malaria transmission by 26.31 and 51.53 % when compared to inert and deterrent LLINs, respectively (Figure 1A and 1B).

Effect of physiological and behavioral resistance

We simulated the effect of attractive LLIN in various scenario of physiological and behavioral insecticide resistance in vectors. Here, we define physiological resistance as the ability to survive feeding attempt in a dwelling equipped with an LLIN. In our model, the probability to survive a feeding attempt equals the probability to successfully take a blood-meal (conditioned on not being diverted; Figure 6). Our simulations show that whatever the level of physiological resistance in the vector population, attractive LLINs are always most efficient than inert or deterrent LLINs (Figure 2A and 2B) but the reduction in transmission lowered as resistance increased. In the absence of physiological resistance (i.e. 0% survival), all other parameters being equals (Table 1), we can expect to reduce malaria transmission by 45.55% and 71.93% with moderately (pLLIN=0.6) and highly attractive (pLLIN=0.7) LLINs, respectively, when compared to an inert LLIN (Figure 2A). When compared to a deterrent LLIN in the same conditions (no resistance), an inert LLIN is exepcted to reduce malaria transmission by 54.24% while moderately (pLLIN=0.6) and highly attractive (pLLIN=0.7) are expected to reduce malaria transmissiion by 75.08% and 87.15%, respectively. When resistance is maximal, attractive LLINs provide a better community protection but with moderate reduction of transmission ranging from 6.96% to 13.37% (Figure 2A) against inert LLINs and from 16.01% to 21.79% against deterrent LLINs (Figure 2B).

Then, we analyzed the effect of behavioral resistances on the efficacy of attractive LLINs. We simulated an increasing level of indoor escaping by varying the value of the diversion probability D_p in our model (Figure X). A diverted mosquito postpones host-seeking to next night. The simulations showed that whatever the level of indoor escaping, attractive LLIN are always better than inert and attractive ones to reduce malaria transmission. As indoor escaping increased, the relative efficacy of attractive LLINs decreased. When compared to an inert LLIN, a moderately attractive LLIN is expected to reduce malaria transmission by 12.81% (high resistance, 100% escaping) to 28.57(lo) % (no resistance, 0% escaping) while a highly attractive LLIN is expected to reduce malaria transmission by 25.88% (high resistance, 100% escaping) to 49.01% (no resistance, 0% escaping) (Figure 3A). When compared to a deterrent LLIN with high resistance (100% escaping), inert and attractive LLINs are expected to reduce malaria transmission by 14.76-36.82 % and by 37.58-36.82%, respectively, in absence of resistance (0% escaping; Figure 3B).

Mosquito vectors of malaria are able to change their behavior to be active for blood searching in spaces and at times when and where people are not using LLINs [refs]. We simulated this spatial-temporal avoidance of LLIN by varying parameter Π that is the proportion of human exposure to bite that occurs while LLINs are in use, or in other terms, the proportion of total bites prevented by using an LLIN. When spatial-temporal avoidance increases, Π decreases. Whatever the rate of spatial-temporal avoidance, attractive LLIN allowed to reduce malaria transmission compared to inert (Figure 4A) and deterrent (Figure 4B) LLINs. As spatial-temporal avoidance increases, the benefit of using attractive LLIN decreases. By 50% of spatial-temporal avoidance, attractive LLINs are expected to reduce malaria transmission by 21.7-38.75% when compared to inert LLINs and by 44.39-56.5% compared to deterrent LLINs. When compared to a deterent LLIN with 50% of spatial-temporal avoidance, an inert LLIN is expected to reduce malaria transmission by 28.97%.

We performed an uncertainty and sensitivity analysis of the model to assess uncertainty of our results due to others input parameters. We therefore defined plausible ranges of values for these parameters (Table 1) and realized a Latin Hypercube Sampling (500 samples) used to run the model [Chalom 2012 arxiv]. Outputs of the models resulting from this sampling showed that attractive LLIN remains always better than inert LLINs for reducing malaria transmission (95 % credible interval of the reduction of transmission was [ , ]; figure S-X). However, our analysis indicated that the results of transmission reduction are highly sensitive to the duration of the gonotrophic cycle (Partial Inclination Coefficient PIC = -6.08 [-5.19, -6.83]) of the vectors and to the parasite extrinsic incubation period (PIC = 1.53 [1.92, 1.22]) (table S-X). This indicates that relative efficacy of attractive LLINs decreases as the duration of the gonotrophic cycle increases and increases when the duration of the extrinsic incubation period increases (figure S-X). The model is also highly sensitive to the baseline daily survival rate (PIC = 35.76 [-5.19, -5.19]) and post-bite mortality in a hut equiped with an LLIN (PIC = 23.71 [26.58, 20.88]). The model is slightly but significantly sensitive to pre-bite mortality in a hut without LLIN (PIC = -38.26 [-25.18, -54.25]).
